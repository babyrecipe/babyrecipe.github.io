How Clean is the Air inside your Home?

December 27, 2017 Leave a Comment

As parents, we often talk to our kids about the importance of gratitude. We teach them to thank others and also to be grateful for all the blessings we have. We tell them that we are lucky to have our health, our family, food to eat and so on. However, we often forget a very basic blessing, one without which life ceases to exist – the air we breathe. Have you ever considered: How Clean is the Air inside your Home?

Air is something we take for granted – after all, it is all around us. While we go the extra mile to ensure that the food and water we feed our children are absolutely pure and healthy, the air we breathe rarely gets that consideration. Just ask yourself – have you thought about whether the air your children are breathing may actually be hurting their health?

Why Indoor Air gets Polluted

When one talks of bad or unhealthy air, it generally conjures up images of heavy smoke coming out of factories or vehicle exhausts. It doesn’t occur to us in our wildest dreams that the air inside our homes – which we consider safe and pure – could be just as bad or even worse than the air outdoors. Here are some reasons why this happens:

  • Pollutants from the outside getting trapped within the house
  • Dust mites, mould and mildew
  • Second hand smoke from cigarettes
  • Use of solid fuels for cooking like wood, coal etc.

Why Children are at Risk

Children spend a lot of their time at home, particularly during cold weather or school holidays, which makes them more exposed to the air pollution indoors. Due to their lower body weight, the pollutants tend to affect them to a greater extent than adults, even affecting their brains. Children also breathe at a higher rate, which means the pollutants have a better chance of entering their systems.

We are aware that children’s immune systems are still developing, which already increases their vulnerability. However, those with a medical condition related to the heart or lungs are at a higher risk. The American Lung Association reports a 73% increase in cases of pediatric asthma. The UNICEF claims that 600,000 children die every year, from causes related to air pollution.

Finding a Solution for Indoor Air Pollution

With facts as scary as these, it is every parent’s responsibility to ensure that their kids have the best quality air to breathe – the most vital component for life. While indoor air pollution may seem like an inescapable fact of life, there are ways to work around this problem:

  • Making sure that VOC and lead-free paint is used within your home
  • Opting for organic household cleaners as far as possible
  • Limiting the use of air fresheners and similar sprays
  • Having a no-tolerance policy for smoking in the house
  • Ensuring regular maintenance to take care of mold and mildew
  • Installing a good quality air purifier
  • Setting up air-purifying plants
